# MA102 — Calculus & Optimization Basics

## Why This Matters

Every model in this program "learns" by adjusting its parameters to reduce some error measure — and the mechanism that decides *which direction* to adjust them in is a derivative. Gradient descent, backpropagation, and basically every optimization technique in ML/DL are calculus applied to functions with thousands or millions of inputs. Without this course, DL101's backprop-from-scratch work becomes a memorized recipe instead of something you actually understand, and every "why is my model not converging" debugging session later becomes guesswork instead of informed diagnosis.

## Prerequisites

- **MA101 (Linear Algebra for ML)** — gradients are vectors, and the multivariable calculus in this course leans directly on vector/matrix intuition from MA101.
- **CS101 (Programming Foundations)** — comfortable with NumPy for implementing derivatives and gradient descent numerically, not just symbolically.

## Where This Is Used Later

- **ML101 (Classical Machine Learning)** — linear/logistic regression are trained via exactly the gradient descent mechanics built in this course.
- **ML103 (Model Evaluation & Experimentation)** — understanding loss landscapes and convergence behavior assumes this course's optimization foundations.
- **DL101 (Neural Networks From Scratch)** — backpropagation *is* the chain rule from this course, applied systematically across layers; you cannot implement it by hand without this.
- **NLP103 (LLMs: Pretraining & Fine-Tuning)** — understanding why training large models is hard (vanishing/exploding gradients, learning rate schedules) traces directly back to the concepts built here.

## Full Topic Breakdown

- **Single-Variable Derivatives**
  - The derivative as instantaneous rate of change / slope of the tangent line
  - Basic differentiation rules: power rule, product rule, quotient rule
  - The chain rule — composing functions and differentiating the composition
  - Higher-order derivatives (a first look, mainly for the Hessian later)
- **Multivariable Calculus**
  - Partial derivatives — holding all but one variable constant
  - The gradient vector — the direction of steepest ascent
  - The Jacobian — generalizing the gradient to vector-valued functions
  - The Hessian — second-order information, and what it tells you about curvature
- **Optimization**
  - What "optimization" means: finding inputs that minimize (or maximize) a function
  - Critical points, local vs. global minima/maxima, saddle points
  - Convexity — what makes a function "easy" to optimize, and why most real loss functions aren't fully convex
  - Gradient descent: the algorithm, the update rule, learning rate, convergence behavior
  - Variants: stochastic gradient descent, mini-batch gradient descent (conceptual introduction)
- **Putting It Together: Toward Backpropagation**
  - Composed functions as computational graphs
  - Applying the chain rule systematically through a graph (forward pass / backward pass framing)
  - Numerical differentiation vs. symbolic differentiation vs. automatic differentiation — what each is, and why ML frameworks use autodiff

## Weekly Study Plan

### Week 1 — Derivatives & Rates of Change

**Topics Covered:**
- The derivative as slope of the tangent line / instantaneous rate of change
- Derivatives from first principles (the limit definition) — enough to understand where the rules come from
- Basic differentiation rules: power rule, sum rule, product rule, quotient rule
- Interpreting a derivative's sign and magnitude (increasing/decreasing, how steeply)

**Tools / Technologies Used:**
- Python 3.x, NumPy, Matplotlib (for plotting functions and tangent lines)

**Key Techniques / Concepts to Internalize:**
- A derivative isn't just "a formula you apply" — it's a specific question ("how fast is this function changing right here?") that has a formula as its answer. Keep coming back to that question when a rule feels arbitrary.
- The sign of a derivative tells you the direction a function is currently heading — this is the single fact that gradient descent (Week 4) is built entirely on.
- Derivatives are local — they describe behavior at a single point, not the function's global shape. A function can have a zero derivative at a point that's neither its highest nor lowest value overall (this becomes important with saddle points later).

**Practical Tips:**
- Common mistake: treating differentiation rules as disconnected facts to memorize instead of noticing the power rule, product rule, etc. all answer the same underlying question in different situations.
- You understood this week if you can sketch, freehand, roughly what the derivative of a simple curve (like a parabola) looks like as its own graph, without computing anything.
- Don't skip plotting. Seeing a function and its derivative side by side is what makes "the derivative is the slope" stick as intuition rather than a sentence you can recite.

### Week 2 — Chain Rule & Differentiation Rules in Practice

**Topics Covered:**
- The chain rule: differentiating composed functions `f(g(x))`
- Recognizing composition in real expressions (not always obviously written as `f(g(x))`)
- Combining chain rule with product/quotient rules on more realistic multi-step expressions
- A first, informal look at numerical differentiation (finite differences) as a sanity-check tool

**Tools / Technologies Used:**
- Python 3.x, NumPy, SymPy (for symbolic differentiation checks)

**Key Techniques / Concepts to Internalize:**
- The chain rule is the single most important rule in this entire course for where this program is headed — backpropagation is nothing more than the chain rule applied repeatedly through a deep composition of functions. Getting genuinely comfortable with it now pays off directly in DL101.
- Spotting composition is a skill, not automatic — `sin(x^2)` is obviously composed, but something like `(3x + 1)^5` is just as much a composition (outer function: "raise to the 5th power," inner function: "3x + 1") even though it doesn't look like it at first glance.
- Numerical differentiation (`(f(x+h) - f(x)) / h` for small `h`) is a useful sanity check for your by-hand derivatives, but it's an approximation with its own error — it's a debugging tool here, not a replacement for understanding the actual rules.

**Practical Tips:**
- Common mistake: applying the chain rule but forgetting to multiply by the derivative of the *inner* function — a very common dropped term that produces a plausible-looking but wrong answer.
- You understood this week if you can take any function that's a composition of 2–3 steps and correctly identify "what's the outer function, what's the inner function" before differentiating anything.
- Use SymPy to check your hand-worked derivatives this week, but do the hand-working first — checking your own reasoning against a symbolic tool is far more valuable than letting the tool do the thinking.

**Recommended Resources:**
- 📺 YouTube: 3Blue1Brown — "Essence of Calculus," Chapter 4 (Visualizing the chain rule and product rule)
- 📘 Book: *Mathematics for Machine Learning* — Chapter 5.2–5.3 (Partial Differentiation and Gradients — early chain rule sections)
- 📄 Docs: [SymPy — differentiation](https://docs.sympy.org/latest/tutorials/intro-tutorial/calculus.html)
- 🤖 AI Prompt: "Give me 4 composed functions of increasing complexity (like sin(x^2), (3x+1)^5, e^(-x^2), etc.). For each, ask me to identify the outer and inner functions before differentiating, then check my chain rule application step by step."

**This Week's Mini Exercise:**

### Week 3 — Partial Derivatives & Gradients

**Topics Covered:**
- Partial derivatives: differentiating a multivariable function with respect to one variable, holding others constant
- The gradient vector: collecting all partial derivatives into one vector
- Geometric meaning of the gradient — the direction of steepest ascent, and why its negative is the direction of steepest descent
- Directional derivatives (conceptual introduction) — rate of change in an arbitrary direction, not just along an axis

**Tools / Technologies Used:**
- Python 3.x, NumPy, Matplotlib (contour plots and 3D surface plots), SymPy

**Key Techniques / Concepts to Internalize:**
- A partial derivative is exactly the single-variable derivative from Week 1, just applied while pretending every other variable is a constant — nothing new is happening mechanically, only the bookkeeping changes.
- The gradient pointing in the direction of steepest *ascent* (not descent) is the detail that trips people up later — gradient descent (Week 4) works by moving in the *negative* gradient direction, and this needs to be a reflex, not something re-derived each time.
- Contour plots are the single best visualization for building gradient intuition — the gradient at any point is always perpendicular to the contour line passing through that point, pointing toward higher contours.

**Practical Tips:**
- Common mistake: computing a gradient correctly but then moving in the positive gradient direction when trying to minimize something — always double check the sign before writing an update rule.
- You understood this week if, looking at a contour plot of a function, you can point to roughly where the gradient vector would point at several different locations, without computing anything.
- Spend real time on 3D surface + contour plots together this week (same function, two views) — connecting the "hill" picture to the "flat map with elevation lines" picture is what makes the gradient concept fully click.

**Recommended Resources:**
- 📺 YouTube: 3Blue1Brown — "Essence of Calculus," Chapter 10 (Gradients, and partial derivatives)
- 📘 Book: *Mathematics for Machine Learning* — Chapter 5.4–5.5 (Gradients of Vector-Valued Functions, Gradients of Matrices)
- 📄 Docs: [Khan Academy — Partial derivatives and gradient](https://www.khanacademy.org/math/multivariable-calculus/multivariable-derivatives)
- 🤖 AI Prompt: "Give me a simple 2-variable function like f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 or a slightly more interesting one with a saddle point. Have me compute the gradient by hand at 3 different points, then check my work and have me interpret what direction the gradient points at each location on a contour plot."

**This Week's Mini Exercise:**
Pick a 2-variable function (e.g. `f(x, y) = x^2 + 2y^2`), compute its gradient symbolically by hand, then use Matplotlib to draw a contour plot of the function with gradient vectors (as arrows) overlaid at a grid of points — confirming visually that every arrow points "uphill" and perpendicular to the local contour line.

### Week 4 — Gradient Descent

**Topics Covered:**
- Critical points: where the gradient is zero, and what that could mean (minimum, maximum, or saddle point)
- The gradient descent algorithm: initialize, compute gradient, step in the negative gradient direction, repeat
- The learning rate — what it controls, and what happens when it's too large or too small
- Convergence behavior: what it looks like when gradient descent is working, oscillating, or diverging
- Stochastic and mini-batch gradient descent — a conceptual introduction (why you wouldn't always use the full dataset's gradient)

**Tools / Technologies Used:**
- Python 3.x, NumPy, Matplotlib

**Key Techniques / Concepts to Internalize:**
- Gradient descent is a genuinely simple algorithm dressed up in intimidating language — "repeatedly take a small step in the direction that decreases the function" is the entire idea; everything else is refinement of that one sentence.
- The learning rate is the single most important hyperparameter in this whole course to develop intuition for — too large and the algorithm overshoots and diverges; too small and it converges so slowly it's impractical. You should be able to recognize both failure modes on a plot of the loss over iterations.
- Stochastic/mini-batch gradient descent exist for a practical reason, not a theoretical one: computing the exact gradient over an entire large dataset every single step is often too expensive, so you trade some noise in the gradient estimate for many more, faster update steps. This tradeoff reappears constantly from ML101 onward.

**Practical Tips:**
- Common mistake: picking a learning rate by guessing once and never revisiting it when the loss curve is clearly diverging or barely moving — treat a bad loss curve as direct, immediate feedback to adjust the learning rate, not as a mysterious failure.
- You understood this week if you can implement gradient descent on a simple function from scratch, plot the loss over iterations, and correctly diagnose (from the plot alone) whether your learning rate is too high, too low, or reasonable.
- Visualize the actual path gradient descent takes on a contour plot, not just the loss-over-time curve — seeing the zig-zag or smooth descent path builds intuition the loss curve alone doesn't.

**Recommended Resources:**
- 📺 YouTube: StatQuest — "Gradient Descent, Step-by-Step"
- 📘 Book: *Mathematics for Machine Learning* — Chapter 7.1 (Optimization Using Gradient Descent)
- 📄 Docs: [scikit-learn — SGD conceptual overview](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/sgd.html) (read the intro/conceptual sections, not the API details)
- 🤖 AI Prompt: "Give me a simple 1D or 2D function to minimize with gradient descent. Have me pick a learning rate and manually trace the first 3–4 steps by hand, then tell me whether my chosen learning rate seems too high, too low, or reasonable based on the behavior, and why."

**This Week's Mini Exercise:**
Implement gradient descent from scratch (NumPy only) to find the minimum of `f(x) = x^2 - 4x + 4` (or a function of your choosing), running it with three different learning rates (one clearly too small, one reasonable, one clearly too large) and plotting the loss-over-iterations curve for all three on the same axes to visually compare the behavior.

### Week 5 — Jacobians, Hessians & Convexity

**Topics Covered:**
- The Jacobian: generalizing the gradient to functions that output vectors, not just scalars
- The Hessian: the matrix of second-order partial derivatives, and what it tells you about a function's curvature at a point
- Using the Hessian to classify critical points (minimum, maximum, saddle point) via its eigenvalues (tying back directly to MA101)
- Convex vs. non-convex functions — what convexity guarantees (a local minimum is the global minimum), and why most real loss functions in deep learning are not convex

**Tools / Technologies Used:**
- Python 3.x, NumPy, SymPy, Matplotlib

**Key Techniques / Concepts to Internalize:**
- The Jacobian is not a new concept, just an extension — it's "a matrix where each row is the gradient of one output with respect to all inputs," which becomes directly relevant when you later work with layers that map vectors to vectors (every neural network layer, essentially).
- The Hessian's eigenvalues (straight from MA101 Week 5) tell you the type of critical point: all-positive means a local minimum, all-negative means a local maximum, mixed signs mean a saddle point. This is a genuinely satisfying moment where two courses' material combines into one useful tool.
- Non-convexity isn't a flaw in deep learning — it's an accepted reality. Understanding *why* loss landscapes for neural networks are non-convex (many parameters, many symmetric equivalent solutions, saddle points) reframes a lot of "why doesn't my model just find the best answer" confusion you'll have later into something expected, not broken.

**Practical Tips:**
- Common mistake: assuming gradient descent always finds *the* global minimum — it finds *a* local minimum (or gets stuck near a saddle point), and for non-convex functions that's a real, expected limitation, not a bug in your implementation.
- You understood this week if, given a Hessian matrix's eigenvalues at a critical point, you can immediately classify that point as a min, max, or saddle without additional computation.
- Don't try to compute Hessians by hand for anything beyond 2 variables — use SymPy or NumPy for anything larger and focus your effort on interpreting what the result means.

**Recommended Resources:**
- 📺 YouTube: 3Blue1Brown — "Essence of Calculus," Chapter 10 continued (or search "Hessian matrix intuition" for a focused explainer)
- 📘 Book: *Mathematics for Machine Learning* — Chapter 5.6–5.9 (Higher-Order Derivatives) and Chapter 7 intro (Convexity)
- 📄 Docs: [Wikipedia — Hessian matrix, "Second derivative test" section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hessian_matrix) (a solid, precise reference for this specific piece)
- 🤖 AI Prompt: "Give me a 2-variable function with a saddle point. Have me compute the Hessian at that point by hand, find its eigenvalues, and classify the critical point — then confirm whether my classification is correct and explain what the saddle point looks like geometrically."

**This Week's Mini Exercise:**
Take a 2-variable function with at least one saddle point (e.g. `f(x, y) = x^2 - y^2`), compute the Hessian symbolically, evaluate it at the critical point(s), find the eigenvalues using NumPy, and classify each critical point — then confirm your classification visually with a 3D surface plot.

### Week 6 — Putting It Together: Toward Backpropagation

**Topics Covered:**
- Representing composed functions as computational graphs (nodes = operations, edges = data flow)
- Forward pass: computing a function's output by walking the graph forward
- Backward pass: applying the chain rule systematically, walking the graph backward, to get gradients with respect to every intermediate variable
- Numerical differentiation vs. symbolic differentiation vs. automatic differentiation — what distinguishes each, and why ML frameworks (PyTorch, etc.) use autodiff specifically

**Tools / Technologies Used:**
- Python 3.x, NumPy (implementing a tiny autodiff system from scratch), SymPy (for symbolic comparison)

**Key Techniques / Concepts to Internalize:**
- Backpropagation is not a separate algorithm from the chain rule — it *is* the chain rule, applied systematically to a computational graph, computing each intermediate gradient exactly once and reusing it. This week's entire goal is making that sentence feel obvious rather than impressive-sounding.
- Automatic differentiation isn't "approximating" derivatives (that's numerical differentiation) and isn't manually deriving a symbolic formula (that's symbolic differentiation) — it's mechanically applying exact differentiation rules at each small operation in the graph and composing them via the chain rule. This is *why* it's both exact and efficient, unlike the alternatives.
- Building even a tiny autodiff system by hand (a handful of operations: add, multiply, power) is disproportionately valuable — it's the single best way to guarantee DL101's "neural network from scratch" backprop implementation isn't just copied code you don't actually understand.

**Practical Tips:**
- Common mistake: thinking of the forward and backward pass as two unrelated procedures — the backward pass literally depends on values computed during the forward pass (this is why frameworks need to "remember" the forward computation).
- You understood this week if you can take a small expression like `f(x) = (x + 2) * (x - 1)`, manually build its computational graph, and compute the gradient at a specific `x` by walking the graph backward, getting the same answer as differentiating symbolically.
- This week is intentionally the hardest integration point in the course — if it doesn't click immediately, that's normal; revisit the chain rule (Week 2) and gradients (Week 3) rather than pushing through confused, since this concept is the direct foundation of DL101's entire first project.

**Recommended Resources:**
- 📺 YouTube: Andrej Karpathy — "The spelled-out intro to neural networks and backpropagation: building micrograd" (this video builds almost exactly the tiny autodiff system this week asks for)
- 📘 Book: *Mathematics for Machine Learning* — Chapter 5.6 (Backpropagation and Automatic Differentiation)
- 📄 Docs: [PyTorch — "A Gentle Introduction to torch.autograd"](https://pytorch.org/tutorials/beginner/blitz/autograd_tutorial.html) (read conceptually, don't worry about the PyTorch-specific API yet)
- 🤖 AI Prompt: "Give me a small composed expression like (x + 2) * (x - 1) or similar. Have me draw out its computational graph by hand (describing nodes and edges), do the forward pass at a specific x value, then do the backward pass step by step to compute the gradient — checking my work against the symbolic derivative at the end."

**This Week's Mini Exercise:**
Build a minimal "autodiff" system in Python: a small `Value` class that wraps a number and, when you use `+`, `*`, or `**` on it, builds up a computational graph and can later compute gradients via a `.backward()` method walking that graph in reverse. Test it against SymPy's symbolic derivative on a few small expressions to confirm the gradients match exactly.

## Main Project

**Project: Gradient Descent Visualizer + Linear Regression From Scratch**

An interactive-enough (even if just multiple saved plots, not a live UI) tool that both visualizes gradient descent behavior on a 2D loss surface and uses it to fit a real linear regression model from scratch, with no ML libraries.

**Requirements:**
- Implement gradient descent from scratch (NumPy only) to fit `y = mx + b` to a synthetic, slightly noisy dataset by minimizing mean squared error.
- Track and plot the loss value at every iteration (the loss-over-time curve).
- Visualize the fitted line's evolution over the course of training — e.g., a plot showing the regression line at several checkpoints (early, middle, converged) overlaid on the data.
- Visualize the gradient descent path itself on a contour plot of the loss surface (loss as a function of `m` and `b`), showing the trajectory from initialization to convergence.
- Run and compare at least 3 different learning rates, clearly showing (via the loss curves) one that diverges, one that converges slowly, and one that converges well.

**Stretch Goals:**
- Implement mini-batch gradient descent as an alternative and compare its loss curve's noisiness to full-batch gradient descent on the same data.
- Extend the model to a simple 2-feature linear regression (`y = m1*x1 + m2*x2 + b`) and visualize the loss surface as a 3D plot instead of 2D.
- Compare your from-scratch fitted `m` and `b` against scikit-learn's `LinearRegression` on the same dataset to confirm they closely match.

**What "Done" Looks Like:**
- The full pipeline runs end-to-end on synthetic data you generate, with no external ML libraries used for the actual fitting.
- You have clear, labeled plots for: the loss curve across learning rates, the fitted line's evolution, and the gradient descent path on the loss surface's contour plot.
- You can explain, without notes, why the chosen "good" learning rate works better than the too-high and too-low ones, referencing the actual plots.
- Code is organized into functions, pushed to this repo's `Projects/` folder with a README containing the key plots.
